Addressing Your Concerns

1. Purpose Beyond "Bringing Manufacturing Back"

The tariffs aim to boost domestic production, reduce trade deficits, protect national security, and raise revenue. Cass’s The Case for a Strategic Tariff argues manufacturing is vital for jobs, innovation, and resilience, protecting industries like steel and semiconductors.

2. Tariffs as Negotiation Leverage

Tariffs are used as a "stick" for better trade terms, as you noted. High rates (e.g., 145% on China) push deals, like the UK’s 10% tariff agreement or Japan’s $550B investment. Fluctuating rates are negotiation tactics, later reduced (e.g., to 30% and 10% post-truce).

3. Trade Deficits

You’re right that deficits aren’t inherently "losing money." But Cass and the administration see the $1.2T goods deficit (2024) as a loss of wages (see the 50-year productivity-wage gap) where $30-per-hour jobs were replaced with $5-per-hour jobs in China, and as a sign of dependency, per the April 2025 "national emergency" declaration: White House Fact Sheet.

4. Fluctuating Rates

Shifting rates (15% to 145%) seem chaotic but are strategic, e.g., China’s 145% tariff included a 20% "fentanyl" component, later cut to 30%. This risks market turmoil, as seen in the 2025 crash: Reuters.

Critique and Context

Messaging can feel simplistic (e.g., Trump’s X posts). Critics warn tariffs raise prices ($1,300/household, 2025). Cass’s vision bets on long-term industrial gains despite short-term pain.
